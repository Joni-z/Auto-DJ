

AutoDJ: A Music Information Retrieval System for Automatic DJ Transitions
Zhizhe
Music, the Mind and Artificial Intelligence
New York University
Spring 2026



AutoDJ is a local web application that renders an automatic transition between two user-provided audio tracks. The system combines beat tracking, tempo normalization, chroma-based key estimation, Camelot Wheel harmonic compatibility, and equal-power crossfading. The goal is not to replace a human DJ, but to demonstrate how music information retrieval (MIR) techniques can support a practical creative workflow. The implementation consists of a FastAPI audio engine and a React interface for uploading tracks, controlling transition parameters, previewing the result, and downloading the rendered mix.

Introduction
DJ mixing is a real-time musical practice built around timing, phrasing, timbre, and harmonic continuity. A smooth transition usually requires the incoming track to be tempo-aligned with the outgoing track, introduced at a musically meaningful phrase boundary, and blended without abrupt level changes. Human DJs acquire these skills through years of listening and repeated practice, learning to identify phrase breaks by ear, adjust pitch in real time, and sculpt a blend that feels inevitable rather than mechanical. AutoDJ asks how far a compact music information retrieval (MIR) pipeline can travel down the same path using only signal processing and a few well-known psychoacoustic rules.

The goal of this project is twofold. First, it serves as a working demonstration of the core MIR techniques introduced over the semester-beat tracking, chroma-based key estimation, time stretching, and tonal compatibility-assembled into a single coherent tool. Second, it is designed to be practically useful: a person without DJ software or training should be able to upload two songs, press a button, and receive a listenable handoff. Making the system's decisions visible in an analysis panel was treated as a design goal in its own right, so the user can understand and override what the algorithm chose rather than accepting a black-box output.

The project accepts two audio files: Track A (outgoing) and Track B (incoming). It estimates the tempo and key of each file, selects a phrase-aligned cut point near the end of Track A, and modifies only the transition region. Track A's final phrase is gradually time-stretched and optionally pitch-morphed toward Track B, while Track B remains at its original tempo and key throughout. During the overlap, Track B's low-frequency content is introduced first, following a common technique in DJ practice known as a bass swap. The application runs entirely locally so that the user can experiment with different phrase sizes and harmonic settings without relying on cloud processing.

Related Work
The implementation follows core MIR concepts described by Muller, especially onset analysis, beat tracking, and chroma features. Beat tracking remains a difficult task because musical styles vary widely in meter, groove, and production style; Bock, Krebs, and Widmer show that multi-model beat tracking can improve robustness across heterogeneous recordings. Key estimation in AutoDJ is based on the Krumhansl-Schmuckler algorithm, which correlates a song's averaged chroma vector against the tonal hierarchies established in cognitive experiments; the underlying pitch-class salience profiles originate from Krumhansl's foundational psychoacoustic research. AutoDJ uses librosa, a widely used Python MIR library that exposes beat tracking, chroma extraction, time stretching, and pitch shifting in a reproducible research-oriented interface. The most direct precedent is Ishizaki et al.'s full-automatic DJ mixing system, which studies tempo adjustment and user discomfort in automatic transitions.

Track B is not time-stretched; its opening phrase is only level-shaped during the overlap, and the untouched Track B body is appended after the transition.

Key Estimation and Camelot Compatibility
The system estimates key using chroma features. Chroma vectors summarize the energy of the twelve pitch classes independent of octave. AutoDJ averages chroma over time and compares the result against Krumhansl-Schmuckler major and minor profiles. The best-scoring tonic and mode are converted to a Camelot Wheel code.

Camelot compatibility is treated as a simple rule set. Two tracks are compatible when they share the same number, when they are adjacent numbers in the same mode, or when they form a relative major/minor pair under the same number. If harmonic correction is enabled, the backend tests pitch shifts from  to  semitones and selects the smallest shift that makes Track A's transition segment compatible with Track B. The main body of Track B is not pitch-shifted.

Phrase Selection and Crossfading
The transition point is chosen near the final third of Track A while respecting the estimated beat grid. The user can select phrase sizes of 8, 16, or 24 bars, and the backend estimates transition duration from the detected BPM, selected phrase length, and tempo gap between the two tracks. At the beginning of the transition, Track A reaches the target tempo quickly over a short alignment window; Track B enters during this phase and then gradually dominates while Track A is attenuated. The backend separates Track B's opening phrase into a low-frequency band (below 180 Hz) and a higher-frequency remainder, introducing the low band first-a technique borrowed from DJ practice where the kick drum and bass are used to anchor the listener before the full spectral content of the incoming track arrives.

Implementation
The project is organized as a local full-stack application. The backend is a FastAPI service with a /api/mix endpoint. It stores uploads, runs the MIR pipeline, writes a WAV file, and returns a JSON analysis report containing BPM, key, Camelot codes, local transition rates, transition pitch shift, cut time, body resume point, and download URL. The frontend is a Vite React application with two upload decks, transition controls, rendering state, an audio preview, and analysis metrics.

Results and Reflection
AutoDJ works best with music that has a stable beat, clear harmonic content, and phrase lengths close to conventional dance music structures. In those cases-four-on-the-floor techno, house, and pop-adjacent electronic music-the rendered transition reliably aligns the incoming rhythm with the outgoing rhythm and produces a crossfade that is smooth enough to be listenable. The Camelot harmonic correction is effective when the two tracks are already close in key: a one- or two-semitone shift is inaudible at normal listening levels and noticeably reduces clashing overtones. The analysis panel-showing BPM, key, Camelot code, detected shift, and transition timing-proved more useful than anticipated, making it easy to judge whether to re-render with different phrase-size or harmonic settings rather than accepting the first result blindly.

The main limitations are inherited from global MIR assumptions that work on average but fail at the boundaries. A single BPM estimate collapses when a track has a tempo ramp, live rubato, or a breakdown with sparse percussion; the phase vocoder then stretches the material at the wrong rate and the beat grid drifts visibly within the overlap. Chroma-based key estimation is similarly fragile: dense production with many layered samples, modal or atonal harmony, or aggressively detuned synthesizers can push the Krumhansl-Schmuckler dot product toward an incorrect tonic. Large pitch shifts (beyond three semitones) introduce phase-vocoder artifacts-a characteristic metallic shimmer-that become obvious on sustained tones or vocals. The phrase-selection logic assumes 4/4 meter and regular eight-bar structures, so it cannot identify a bridge, a breakdown, or an extended outro and may choose a transition point inside a structural moment rather than at a phrase boundary.

On reflection, the project demonstrated clearly that the gap between analytically correct and perceptually smooth is significant in automatic mixing. The tempo ratio and key shift can both be mathematically correct while the result still sounds forced if the timbral energy of the two tracks is mismatched or if the phrase boundary was slightly misjudged. This gap is precisely what a skilled human DJ closes through their ear, and it is the hardest part to automate without higher-level structural analysis.

Future improvements would include downbeat detection, structural segmentation, source separation for stem-aware EQ transitions, loudness normalization using LUFS, and an interactive timeline where the user can move the transition point before rendering.

Conclusion
AutoDJ demonstrates how MIR can turn musical analysis into a usable creative tool. By combining tempo estimation, key detection, harmonic rules, and crossfade rendering, the system produces a complete automatic transition from two audio files. Its value lies in making the algorithmic decisions inspectable and adjustable, which keeps the user in the creative loop while automating the mechanical parts of beat matching and rendering.
